You got the job.

Let me say that again — you actually got the job.

After the applications, the interviews, the prayer sessions, the anxious waiting, the NYSC stress — you finally got the offer letter. Your family celebrated. Your friends sent fire emojis. Your mum called relatives you haven't spoken to in three years just to announce it.

And then Monday came.

And you sat at that desk, in that office, surrounded by people who all seemed to know things that nobody ever taught you. There's a whole invisible game being played around you — and you cannot see the rules, cannot find the scorecard, cannot figure out why the hardest-working person in the room is sometimes the most invisible.

Am I doing this wrong? Is everyone else struggling too and just hiding it? Why does my manager's feedback never make sense? Why do I feel like a fraud every single day?

You go home exhausted — not from the work itself, but from the performance. From spending eight hours watching your every word, second-guessing every email before you send it, rehearsing conversations in your head that never go the way you planned.

You've Googled it. You've watched YouTube videos. You've read LinkedIn posts about "thriving in your career." And every single time, the advice feels like it was written for someone in a glass tower in San Francisco — not for you, in your office in Lagos or Abuja or Port Harcourt, where your oga's mood can change the entire floor's temperature before 9am.

Maybe I'm just not cut out for this. Maybe I made a mistake thinking I could do this job.

Stop.

That is not true. And deep down you already know that.

You are not failing because you're not smart enough. You are not failing because you're not working hard enough. You are struggling because you were handed a job without a rulebook — and everyone just assumed you'd figure it out.

You spent years preparing for the interview. Nobody spent five minutes preparing you for what happens after.

Drop everything you are doing now and read every single word I am about to say.

Because I'm about to share with you a simple field guide that completely changed how I show up at work — and how work shows up for me.

Here's something nobody talks about enough.

The people who thrive in corporate Nigeria — the ones who get noticed, get promoted, get chosen for the interesting projects — most of them didn't figure it out alone. Somewhere along the line, someone pulled them aside and told them how things actually work. A mentor. An older cousin. A godparent in the industry. Someone who said: "Let me tell you what nobody officially tells you."

That conversation is the unfair advantage most people never get.

Until now.

Let me take you back to my first week at my job.

I had worked so hard to get there. Best results in my department at university. Three months post-NYSC and I already had an offer. People in my family were treating me like I had done something remarkable. I walked into that office on my first Monday like I was ready.

I was not ready.

By the end of week two, I was already questioning everything.

My manager would give me feedback and I genuinely could not tell if I was doing well or badly. He'd say things like "good effort, but let's revisit this" — and I'd smile and nod and walk back to my desk with absolutely no idea what "revisit" meant. Revisit how? Revisit when? What was wrong with it?

I watched a colleague — someone who had only been there three months longer than me — get chosen for a client presentation. I had worked twice as hard that week. I had stayed late. I had triple-checked everything I submitted. She got the presentation. I got silence.

What does she know that I don't know?

I started going home with a tightness in my chest that I couldn't explain. Not from the work — from the watching. From trying to decode a social environment that had no official manual. From feeling like there was a game being played around me that everyone else understood.

My confidence started cracking.

I remember one evening calling my aunty Folake — she's been in corporate Lagos for over 20 years — and just crying. Not dramatically. Just tired crying. The kind where you're not even sure what exactly you're upset about.

She listened. Then she said something I will never forget:

"Kemi. They hired you because of your brain. But what keeps you in that room is different. It's something nobody teaches you in school. The sooner you understand that, the sooner things will shift."

That conversation planted a seed.

But I still didn't know what to do with it. So I did what every overachieving, Google-everything person does. I started searching for answers.

I tried everything.

I watched career advice YouTube channels for weeks. The content was good — genuinely. But it was all built for a corporate world that looks nothing like the Nigerian one. "Schedule a 1:1 with your manager" — my manager barely responds to emails in under two days. "Advocate loudly for yourself in meetings" — I tried that once and someone senior cut me off mid-sentence and nobody flinched. I flinched for two days.

I bought an expensive online career course. I completed it. I learned about "personal branding" and "executive presence." Very interesting in theory. The morning after I finished it I went to the office and still had no idea how to handle my manager's vague feedback or why I kept getting overlooked.

I asked a senior colleague to mentor me. She said yes enthusiastically and then cancelled three meetings in a row. By the fourth time I stopped asking.

I started reading every LinkedIn post from Nigerian corporate professionals. Motivational content. "Your time is coming." "Keep working hard." "Stay consistent." All of it true. None of it useful at 8am on a Monday when you don't know if you're on thin ice with the person who writes your performance review.

I downloaded free "first job survival" PDFs from the internet. They were... fine. Checklists about showing up on time and dressing professionally. Things I already knew. Nobody was talking about what happens inside the walls of a real Nigerian office — the politics, the hierarchy, the invisible signals, the things that actually determine who rises and who stagnates.

Nothing was solving the actual problem.

Then something happened that I didn't expect.

My company hosted a small retirement gathering for one of the directors — Alhaji Bello, who had been in the industry for 34 years. I almost didn't go. I'm not naturally social. But my mum had drilled into me since childhood: "Show your face at the important moments." So I went.

I ended up in a quiet corner of the room, half watching people network, half checking my phone. And this older woman sat down beside me. Madam Ngozi. Late 50s. Former HR director, she told me later. Now consulting for several firms. She had this very calm energy — the kind of person who has seen enough to not be rattled by anything anymore.

She asked me how I was settling in. And I don't know why — maybe it was the third cup of Chapman or just the tiredness — but I told her the truth. I told her I was struggling. That I felt like everyone else had been given a handbook I hadn't received.

She nodded slowly. Like she had heard this before. Many times.

Then she said: "They haven't given you any handbook, my dear. There isn't one. But there is a pattern. And once you see the pattern, you can't unsee it."

She talked for maybe thirty minutes. I stopped eating. I just listened.

She told me that the first year of any corporate job in Nigeria is not really about your output. It's about your positioning. It's about learning to read the room — which voices carry real weight, which tasks get you seen, how to make your contributions visible without appearing to be performing for attention, which would be punished.

She talked about managing upward — how to communicate with your manager in a way that makes them feel informed and respected, even when their communication style is unclear or their feedback is vague. She talked about the specific kind of quiet influence that Nigerian office culture rewards — not the loudest voice, but the most strategically present one.

"The people who move fast in these offices," she said, "are not always the smartest. They are the ones who understood early that the visible work and the invisible work are different things — and they put equal energy into both."

I went home that night and wrote down everything she said.

I started applying it. Not dramatically — just small, deliberate shifts. I began mapping my office relationships differently. I changed how I handled my manager's ambiguous feedback. I found a way to make my work more visible without changing my personality. I stopped rehearsing emails for 45 minutes and started using a simple framework that made every message clear and confident.

The first few weeks felt strange. Like wearing different shoes. Not uncomfortable exactly — just unfamiliar.

Then something shifted.

My manager, for the first time, gave me feedback that was specific and positive. Not vague. Not "good effort." He said my name in a meeting and recommended a piece of my work to a client. I had to stop myself from showing how surprised I was.

Two months later, I was asked to join a project I hadn't been invited to before. One of the project leads told me: "We hear your name a lot in good contexts. We wanted you on this."

I remember sitting in my car after that conversation and just breathing. Oh. So this is what it's supposed to feel like.

My close friend Simi, who works at a different firm in Victoria Island, called me one evening after I shared some of Madam Ngozi's principles with her. She had been having a similar experience — brilliant, hardworking, and completely invisible. Within six weeks of applying what I shared, she told me her manager had nominated her for an internal development programme. "Kemi, what did you do to me? I feel like I can finally breathe at work."

My cousin Bisi tried it too. Fresh out of NYSC, first job in Abuja. She messaged me three weeks in: "My manager called me 'reliable' in a team meeting today. I've never been called reliable before. I've been called smart, I've been called ambitious — but reliable is the one that actually means something here."

That word. Reliable. That's the currency that matters in year one.

I started getting messages. From friends. Friends of friends. People who had somehow heard that I had "figured something out" about surviving the first year. I was sharing notes in WhatsApp chats, over lunch, on phone calls.

And I realised — this information is not widely available. Not for us. Not in a form that actually fits our world. And I needed to change that.

I get too many messages now to respond to individually. And honestly, what I want to share cannot be rushed into a voice note or a quick chat.

So I did what I should have done months ago.

I sat down and wrote everything down properly. The full framework. The tools. The strategies. The exact things Madam Ngozi shared with me, combined with everything I tested, refined, and verified — in a real Nigerian corporate environment, with real results.

I put everything inside one complete guide. The office mapping exercise. The manager decoder. The visibility formula. The scripts for the awkward moments. The weekly routine that keeps you intentional and noticed. All of it — in one place, in language that actually fits our world.
