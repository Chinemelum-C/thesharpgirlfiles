A Lagos Professional Shares the Simple Field Guide That Helped Her Stop Feeling Lost in Her First Corporate Job — and Start Showing Up With Confidence

Published: 14 March 2025  ·  By Kemi  ·  8 min read

You got the job.

Let me say that again — you actually got the job.

After the applications, the interviews, the prayer sessions, the anxious waiting, the NYSC stress — you finally got the offer letter. Your family celebrated. Your friends sent fire emojis. Your mum called relatives you haven't spoken to in three years just to announce it.

And then Monday came.

And you sat at that desk, in that office, surrounded by people who all seemed to know things that nobody ever taught you. There's a whole invisible game being played around you — and you cannot see the rules, cannot find the scorecard, cannot figure out why the hardest-working person in the room is sometimes the most invisible.

Am I doing this wrong? Is everyone else struggling too and just hiding it? Why does my manager's feedback never quite make sense? Why do I feel like a fraud every single day?

You go home exhausted — not from the work itself, but from the watching. From spending eight hours second-guessing every email before you send it, rehearsing conversations in your head that never go the way you planned.

You've Googled it. You've watched the YouTube videos. You've read the LinkedIn posts. And every single time, the advice feels like it was written for someone in a glass tower in San Francisco — not for you, in your office in Lagos or Abuja or Port Harcourt, where your oga's mood can change the entire floor's temperature before 9am.

Maybe I'm just not cut out for this.

Stop.

That thought is not true. And deep down you already know that.

You are not struggling because you are not smart enough. You are not struggling because you are not working hard enough. You are struggling because you were handed a job without a rulebook — and everyone assumed you'd figure it out.

You spent years preparing for the interview. Nobody spent five minutes preparing you for what happens after.

Drop everything you are doing now and read every word I am about to say.

Because I'm about to share the guide that helped me stop surviving my first corporate job — and start actually thriving in it.

Here's something nobody talks about enough.

The people who tend to thrive in corporate Nigeria — the ones who get noticed, get chosen for the good projects, get promoted ahead of schedule — most of them had help. Somewhere along the line, someone pulled them aside and told them how things actually work. A mentor. An older cousin. A godparent in the industry. Someone who said: "Let me tell you what nobody officially tells you."

That conversation is the unfair advantage that most people never get.

Hi. My name is Kemi.

First thing you should know about me: I am not a career coach, I don't have a degree in human resources, and I am not a motivational speaker. I'm a young woman who works in a demanding Nigerian corporate environment, who went through her first year feeling completely lost — and who eventually figured out what was actually going on.

Let me take you back to my first week.

I had worked so hard to get there. Best results in my department at university. Three months post-NYSC and I already had an offer. I walked into that office on my first Monday like I was ready.

I was not ready.

By the end of week two, I was questioning everything.

My manager would give me feedback and I genuinely could not tell if I was doing well or badly. He'd say things like "good effort, but let's revisit this" — and I'd smile and nod and walk back to my desk with no idea what "revisit" meant. Revisit how? Revisit when? What was wrong with it?

I watched a colleague — someone who had only been there three months longer than me — get chosen for a client presentation. I had worked twice as hard that week. I had stayed late. I had triple-checked everything I submitted. She got the presentation. I got silence.

What does she know that I don't know?

I started going home with a tightness in my chest that I couldn't name. Not from the work — from the watching. From trying to decode a social environment that had no official manual.

I remember calling my aunty Folake one evening — she's been in corporate Lagos for over 20 years — and just talking it all out. She listened. Then she said something I haven't forgotten:

"Kemi. They hired you because of your brain. But what keeps you in that room is different. It's something nobody teaches you in school. The sooner you understand that, the sooner things will shift."

That planted a seed. But I still didn't know what to do with it. So I started searching.

I tried everything available to me.

I watched career advice YouTube channels for weeks. The content was genuinely useful — but built for a corporate world that looks nothing like the Nigerian one. "Schedule a 1:1 with your manager" — mine barely responds to emails in under two days. "Advocate loudly for yourself in meetings" — I tried that once and someone senior cut me off mid-sentence. Nobody flinched. I flinched for two days.

I bought an expensive online career course. I completed it. I learned about "personal branding" and "executive presence." Very interesting in theory. The morning after I finished it, I went to the office and still had no idea how to handle my manager's vague feedback or why I kept getting overlooked.

I asked a senior colleague to mentor me. She said yes enthusiastically and then cancelled three meetings in a row. By the fourth time, I stopped asking.

I downloaded free "first job survival" PDFs. Checklists about showing up on time and dressing professionally. Things I already knew. Nobody was talking about what actually happens inside the walls of a real Nigerian office — the hierarchy, the invisible signals, the things that actually determine who rises.

Nothing was addressing the actual problem.

Then something unexpected happened.

My company hosted a small retirement gathering for one of the directors. I almost didn't go — I'm not naturally social. But I went. And I ended up in a quiet corner of the room beside an older woman who sat down next to me. Madam Ngozi. Former HR director, now consulting. Late 50s. The kind of calm that comes from having seen everything.

She asked how I was settling in. And I told her the truth. I said I was struggling. That I felt like everyone else had received a handbook I hadn't.

She nodded slowly. Like she had heard this before.

"There isn't a handbook, my dear. But there is a pattern. And once you see the pattern, you can't unsee it."

She talked for maybe thirty minutes. I stopped eating and just listened.

She told me that the first year of any corporate job in Nigeria is not primarily about your output. It's about your positioning. Learning to read the room. Understanding which voices carry real weight, which tasks get you seen, how to make your contributions visible without appearing to perform for attention — which, in Nigerian office culture, tends to be penalised.

She talked about managing upward — how to communicate with your manager in a way that makes them feel informed and respected, even when their communication style is unclear. She talked about the specific kind of quiet influence that Nigerian office culture tends to reward — not the loudest voice, but the most strategically present one.

"The people who move fast in these offices," she said, "are not always the smartest. They are the ones who understood early that the visible work and the invisible work are different things — and they invested in both."

I went home that night and wrote down everything she said.

I started applying it. Small, deliberate shifts. I began mapping my office relationships differently. I changed how I handled my manager's ambiguous feedback. I found a way to make my work more visible without changing my personality.

A few weeks in, something shifted.

My manager gave me specific, positive feedback for the first time. Not "good effort." He mentioned my work by name in a meeting and recommended it to a client.

A few months later, I was asked to join a project I hadn't been on before. One of the project leads said: "We hear your name in good contexts. We wanted you on this."

I sat in my car after that conversation and just breathed. Oh. So this is what it is supposed to feel like.

Friends started asking what had changed. I started sharing what I had learned — in WhatsApp chats, over lunch, on late-night calls. And I kept getting the same response: why has nobody written this down properly?

So I wrote it down.

I built one complete guide. The office mapping exercise. The manager decoder. The visibility approach. The scripts for the uncomfortable moments. The weekly routine that keeps you intentional and present. All of it — in language that actually fits our world.
